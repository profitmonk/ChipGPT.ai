Here is an uncomfortable fact about a whole class of hardware bugs: you can run your RTL simulation a thousand times, hit 100% functional coverage, watch every assertion pass — and still ship the bug to silicon. Not because you didn't test enough. Because **simulation cannot see it at all.**

The class is clock-domain crossings (CDC), and it's one of the most expensive bug families in modern SoCs — 95% of which now span multiple clock domains.

## What a clock-domain crossing is

A signal generated by logic running on clock A, sampled by logic running on clock B, has *crossed a clock domain*. The two clocks are asynchronous — their edges drift relative to each other. Sooner or later, clock B will sample the signal at the exact moment it's transitioning. The receiving flip-flop goes **metastable**: for a short window its output is neither a clean 0 nor a clean 1, and different downstream gates may resolve it differently.

The fix is well known: never let a raw cross-domain signal fan out. Pass single-bit signals through a two-flop synchronizer (`prim_flop_2sync` and friends); pass multi-bit data through a proper CDC handshake or an async FIFO. Miss one crossing, and you have a latent failure that depends on temperature, voltage, and silicon lottery.

## Why simulation is blind

RTL simulation is **zero-delay**: signals settle instantly within a delta cycle, and every flip-flop sees a clean, fully-resolved value on its clock edge. Metastability — the entire failure mechanism — *does not exist in that model.* The sample always lands cleanly. So a missing synchronizer simulates exactly like a present one. The testbench is happy. Coverage is green. The bug is invisible.

It stays invisible right up until gate-level simulation with back-annotated timing, or until the part is on a bench and fails one board in fifty at the wrong temperature — after hundreds of regression hours and a tape-out are already spent.

A real example: in OpenTitan's root-of-trust SoC, the life-cycle controller's interface to the KMAC crypto accelerator crossed from one clock domain to another without proper synchronization ([lineage around #20979](https://github.com/lowRISC/opentitan/issues/20979)). It was caught in gate-level simulation — *after* it had passed RTL sim. On a less rigorous project it reaches silicon.

## Why assertions and coverage don't save you

It's tempting to think "we'll just write an assertion." But a functional SVA property is evaluated *in the same zero-delay model* — it can't observe metastability either. And coverage measures whether you exercised the logic, not whether the logic is structurally safe across clocks. You can have perfect coverage of a crossing that is fundamentally unsafe.

## What actually catches it: structural analysis

CDC bugs are found by analyzing the **structure** of the design, not its simulated behavior:

1. Enumerate every clock domain in the module.
2. Trace every signal that crosses from one domain to another.
3. For each crossing, check that a recognized synchronizer or handshake sits on the path.
4. Flag the crossings that don't.

No stimulus, no waveform — a static walk of the design's clock topology. It's a different *category* of verification from simulation and from functional formal, and it's the only one that sees this bug. (This is exactly the kind of check commercial CDC tools and lint flows perform; the point is that it must be *part of the flow*, because neither sim nor functional formal substitutes for it.)

## The takeaway

If your sign-off story is "simulation clean + assertions pass + coverage closed," you have a blind spot the size of every clock-domain crossing in the design. The most rigorous teams treat structural CDC analysis as a first-class gate, not an afterthought — because the alternative is finding out in the lab.
